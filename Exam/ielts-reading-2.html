Passage 1

The History of the Bicycle

The bicycle is a widely used form of transport that has undergone significant changes since its inception. The earliest ancestor of the modern bicycle was the 'dandy horse', invented by Baron Karl von Drais in Germany in 1817. This machine had no pedals and was moved by the rider pushing their feet against the ground. It was made almost entirely of wood.

In the 1860s, French inventors Pierre Michaux and Pierre Lallement developed the first bicycle with pedals, known as the 'boneshaker' because of its uncomfortable ride on cobblestone streets. These early bikes had wooden wheels with iron rims. It wasn't until the 1870s that the 'penny-farthing' appeared, featuring a massive front wheel and a tiny rear wheel. While faster, it was notoriously dangerous due to its instability.

The breakthrough came in 1885 with John Kemp Starley's 'Safety Bicycle'. It featured two wheels of equal size and a chain-driven rear wheel. This design, combined with John Boyd Dunlop's invention of the pneumatic (air-filled) tire in 1888, made bicycling accessible and comfortable for the general public, leading to a worldwide 'bicycle craze' in the 1890s.

Passage 2

The Science of Laughter

Laughter is a universal human behavior, yet its biological purpose remains a subject of intense research. From a physiological standpoint, laughter involves the contraction of fifteen facial muscles and an alteration in breathing. When we laugh, the brain releases endorphins, which are natural feel-good chemicals that promote an overall sense of well-being and can even temporarily relieve pain.

Recent studies have shown that laughter can reduce levels of stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline. It also improves cardiovascular health by increasing blood flow and improving the function of blood vessels. Some researchers suggest that a good laugh is equivalent to a mild workout for the heart and lungs.

Beyond physiology, laughter is the "social glue" of humanity. Evolutionary psychologists believe laughter developed long before language as a way to signal safety and strengthen social bonds within a group. It is a powerful tool for communication that transcends cultural boundaries.

Vertical Farming: The Future of Agriculture

[A] By 2050, the world's population is expected to reach nearly 10 billion. As traditional farmland becomes scarce and climate change threatens crop yields, vertical farming offers a potential solution by growing crops in stacked layers in controlled indoor environments.

[B] One major advantage is resource efficiency. Vertical farms use up to 95% less water than traditional farms because water is recycled through specialized systems. Since the environment is completely controlled, no chemical pesticides are needed, making the produce cleaner and healthier.

[C] Vertical farming is particularly beneficial for urban areas. By locating farms within cities, the distance food travels from farm to consumer—known as 'food miles'—is significantly reduced. This ensures fresher produce for city dwellers and lowers carbon emissions associated with transportation.

[D] Despite these benefits, vertical farming faces challenges like high energy consumption. These systems rely on artificial LED lighting and climate control, requiring vast amounts of electricity. However, proponents believe that as technology improves, it will become the cornerstone of sustainable agriculture.
