Passage 1

The Desolenator: producing clean water

Travel around the world and you will soon find that clean water is a luxury. According to the United Nations, more than 800 million people lack access to clean water. In many regions, the problem isn't just a lack of water, but rather that the available water is salty or contaminated. This is where the Desolenator comes in.

Developed by a team in the UK, the Desolenator is a solar-powered device that can turn seawater or contaminated water into drinking water. Unlike large-scale desalination plants that require massive amounts of energy and expensive infrastructure, the Desolenator is small, portable, and runs entirely on solar power. It uses a combination of solar panels and a thermal system to heat the water until it boils.

The steam is then condensed into pure water, while the salt and other impurities are left behind. The device can produce up to 15 litres of water a day, which is enough for a small family's cooking and drinking needs. Because it has no moving parts and doesn't require filters, it is extremely low-maintenance and can last for up to 20 years.

Passage 2

Why we need to protect polar bears

Polar bears are being increasingly threatened by the effects of climate change, but their disappearance could have far-reaching consequences. They are uniquely adapted to the extreme conditions of the Arctic Circle, where temperatures can drop to -40°C. One of their most remarkable adaptations is their ability to survive with high levels of body fat.

Unlike humans, for whom excessive fat often leads to heart disease or diabetes, polar bears remain healthy despite their fat reserves. Researchers have found that this is due to a genetic mutation in a gene called APOB, which helps clear low-density lipoproteins (LDLs) - or "bad" cholesterol - from their blood. Understanding how polar bears manage this could lead to breakthroughs in treating human heart disease.

Furthermore, polar bears are highly intelligent animals. Studies have shown they are capable of complex problem-solving and have been observed using tools, a trait once thought to be unique to humans and primates. If the sea ice continues to melt, these magnificent creatures may lose their hunting grounds and face extinction.

Passage 3

The step pyramid of Djoser

[Paragraph A] The step pyramid of Djoser was the first pyramid built in Ancient Egypt. Constructed around 2670 BC, it marked a significant shift from the traditional mud-brick mastaba tombs to monumental stone architecture. The architect responsible for this masterpiece was Imhotep, a man of such talent that he was later deified as a god of healing.

[Paragraph B] The pyramid was built for King Djoser, the second king of the Third Dynasty. Unlike later pyramids with smooth sides, this structure consists of six tiers or "steps" that rise to a height of 62 metres. It was originally encased in fine white limestone, making it a dazzling sight in the desert sun.

[Paragraph C] Beneath the pyramid lies a labyrinth of tunnels and chambers, spanning nearly 6 kilometres. These were designed to store grave goods and to house the king's family. The burial chamber itself was lined with pink granite, and the entrance was sealed with a massive stone block weighing 3.5 tons.

[Paragraph D] Despite the ingenuity of its design, the pyramid was not immune to tomb robbers. Most of the treasures were stolen centuries ago. However, the structure remains one of the most important archaeological sites in the world, representing the birth of pyramid building.
